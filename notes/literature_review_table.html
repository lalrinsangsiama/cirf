Table 2.1: Theoretical Contributions and Limitations Across Domains
Theoretical Domain Key Contributions Critical Limitations Framework Implications
Innovation Studies

Key Theorists:
Schumpeter, Lundvall, Nelson, Von Hippel
  • Systems thinking: Innovation occurs within networks of relationships
  • Co-creation processes: Value emerges through collaboration
  • User innovation: Innovation originates from practitioners
  • Interactive learning: Knowledge flows between actors
  • Institutional context: Innovation shaped by supporting structures
  • Creative destruction bias: Assumes progress requires destroying existing systems
  • Individual focus: Emphasizes individual inventors over community processes
  • Formal institution bias: Overlooks informal cultural institutions
  • Technological privilege: Marginalizes social and cultural innovations
  • Market orientation: Assumes commercial objectives as primary
  • Innovation-within-tradition: Framework must enable creative preservation
  • Community networks: Recognize informal cultural transmission
  • Multiple objectives: Support cultural alongside economic goals
  • Collective processes: Enable community-controlled innovation
Cultural Economics

Key Theorists:
Bourdieu, Throsby, Klamer, Potts & Cunningham
  • Cultural capital theory: Culture functions as economic asset
  • Multiple value recognition: Cultural value irreducible to economic measures
  • Field autonomy: Cultural production operates through distinct logics
  • Asset mobilization: Communities possess sophisticated cultural resources
  • Value creation: Culture generates economic returns while maintaining integrity
  • Individual accumulation focus: Emphasizes personal cultural capital over collective assets
  • Market assumptions: Assumes markets adequately reward cultural value
  • Power asymmetry blindness: Ignores structural barriers to value capture
  • Dominant culture bias: Privileges established cultural forms
  • Extraction enablement: Creative industries discourse facilitates appropriation
  • Collective ownership: Recognize community cultural assets
  • Protective mechanisms: Prevent cultural appropriation
  • Multidimensional assessment: Capture multiple value forms simultaneously
  • Equitable distribution: Ensure community benefit capture
Resilience Theory

Key Theorists:
Holling, Berkes, Kirmayer, Martin & Sunley
  • Adaptive capacity: Systems maintain function through change
  • Cultural continuity: Identity maintenance as protective factor
  • Traditional knowledge: Indigenous knowledge as sophisticated innovation system
  • Collective resilience: Community wellbeing emerges from cultural practices
  • Dynamic adaptation: Resilience involves beneficial transformation
  • Cultural dimensions undertheorized: Economic resilience literature marginalizes culture
  • Identity instrumentalization: Treats culture as tool rather than foundation
  • Individual psychology focus: Emphasizes personal over collective resilience
  • Static preservation bias: Cultural sustainability frameworks risk museum-ification
  • Epistemological marginalization: Scientific superiority assumptions
  • Identity maintenance: Cultural integrity as non-negotiable requirement
  • Knowledge validation: Recognize traditional knowledge as innovation source
  • Collective focus: Support community-level adaptive capacity
  • Dynamic sustainability: Enable cultural evolution within frameworks
Development Studies

Key Theorists:
Ray, Escobar, Smith, Ostrom, Kretzmann & McKnight
  • Endogenous development: Community agency and resource control
  • Asset-based approaches: Building on existing capabilities
  • Community control: Self-determination as development prerequisite
  • Post-development critique: Challenges colonial development assumptions
  • Indigenous governance: Sophisticated community management systems
  • Implementation gap: Participatory rhetoric without genuine control transfer
  • Scale limitations: Difficulty engaging global markets while maintaining control
  • Institutional hostility: External systems actively undermine community control
  • Capacity assumptions: Underestimates community governance sophistication
  • Isolation risk: Pure endogenous approaches may limit opportunities
  • Operational sovereignty: Embed community control throughout framework
  • Strategic engagement: Enable beneficial external relationships
  • Institutional innovation: Create supportive governance arrangements
  • Capacity recognition: Build on existing community assets
This table synthesizes the comprehensive literature analysis across four theoretical domains, identifying valuable contributions alongside critical limitations that prevent existing frameworks from adequately supporting cultural innovation. The framework implications column translates these insights into specific design requirements for the Cultural Innovation Resilience Framework, demonstrating how theoretical analysis informs practical tool development that addresses identified gaps while building on established scholarly foundations.
